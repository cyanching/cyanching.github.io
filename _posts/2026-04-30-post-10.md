This essay, written with my friends and fellow researchers, examines a major bottleneck in modern science: the loss of knowledge from failed or discontinued research. We argue that these so-called dead ends are often the starting points for iterative improvement, and that preserving them through structured reporting, shared databases and reformed publishing practices could make research more efficient, collaborative and transparent.

Title: The Missing Half of Science: Rethink Negative Results

Authors: Noa Midzic, Cyan Ching, Astrid Canal

If I get a new idea, I often feel that it has probably been done before. Outside of checking places like Google Scholar and similar platforms, it is difficult to know whether something has never been attempted, or whether it was explored and quietly abandoned. Most published articles present successful or at least partially successful results. It is rare to encounter a paper that concludes, “this approach was tested and proven wrong.” There is a quiet contradiction here: if something leads nowhere, why would it be written up at all? It is unlikely to be published in a journal, and outside of that, people rarely share such outcomes publicly. Failure tends to disappear.

For researchers in academia, this is reinforced by incentives. Salaries are modest, positions are limited, and reputation plays a central role in career progression. Professional relationships, recommendations and visibility matter. In such an environment, openly discussing failed ideas or unsuccessful projects can feel like a risk rather than a contribution. As a result, failures are often left undocumented beyond internal notes or conversations.

When scaled to the level of modern science, this becomes a structural flaw. Publicly funded research teams unknowingly repeat experiments that have already failed elsewhere. Science has built vast libraries of success, yet the equally valuable record of failure is fragmented and often lost. Time, expertise and public funding are repeatedly spent rediscovering the same dead ends.

Yet failure is not the opposite of progress. Science advances as much by ruling things out as by discovering what works. A failed experiment can reveal flawed assumptions, limitations in methodology, or conditions under which an idea breaks down. Many failures are more complex than they first appear. An experiment may fail not because the core idea is wrong, but because of the model used, the protocol chosen, the timing, or the way results were measured. Without access to these details, researchers cannot distinguish between a fundamentally weak idea and a poor implementation. Instead of refining earlier attempts, they are forced to start again from zero, losing the opportunity to improve on prior work.

The usual response has been to encourage scientists to publish negative results. While well intentioned, this has not solved the problem. Researchers are rewarded for papers, grants and strong narratives, while publishing unsuccessful work takes additional time and offers little career benefit. Even voluntary or anonymous platforms depend on individuals choosing to contribute work they have already moved on from, which leads to uneven participation.

This suggests that the issue is not individual behaviour, but system design.

If failure is to become part of shared scientific knowledge, responsibility must shift from individual researchers to the institutions that fund research. Public funding bodies could require that unsuccessful or discontinued experimental pathways be documented in a standardised format and deposited into a shared database. In the European Union, this could be integrated into Horizon Europe. In the United States, similar mechanisms could be implemented through agencies such as National Institutes of Health, National Science Foundation, Department of Energy, DARPA and ARPA-H.

Optional reporting tends to produce patchy participation. However, if documentation were tied to grant milestones or final payments, it would become a routine part of completing a project rather than something dependent on goodwill.

The purpose of such a system would not be to catalogue failure in excessive detail, but to capture its essential lessons. Researchers could briefly describe what they attempted, the methods used and why the work was discontinued. These summaries would allow others to quickly assess whether a similar approach is worth pursuing, and under what conditions it might be improved. Sensitive details could be anonymised or released later where necessary.

However, preserving failure is only one part of the problem. The structure of scientific publishing itself also contributes to inefficiency. Current academic publishing favours complete narratives: large, comprehensive studies that present a full story. While convenient, this raises the barrier to publication and encourages researchers to accumulate results before sharing them.

This model also limits how failure is communicated. Even when unsuccessful attempts are included, they are often condensed into minor sections or supplementary material, reducing their visibility and usefulness.

A more effective approach would be to move toward publishing well-documented individual experiments, regardless of outcome. Instead of requiring each team to produce a complete narrative, research could be communicated as a series of precise, reproducible contributions. In this model, no single paper needs to resolve an entire question. Understanding emerges collectively from many smaller contributions.

This shift would also allow evaluation criteria to evolve. Scientific merit would not depend solely on successful outcomes, but also on clarity, rigour and usefulness. A carefully documented failed experiment provides valuable information, prevents duplication of effort and reflects deep engagement with a research problem. This is particularly important for students and early-career researchers, for whom analysing and articulating failure is a key part of scientific development.

These contributions are measurable. Failed experiments can be peer reviewed, cited and built upon. Systematically summarising failed attempts also benefits researchers directly. By analysing why an approach did not work, scientists can identify weaknesses in their assumptions, methods or measurements, receive feedback, and design stronger follow-up experiments. In this way, failure becomes part of an iterative and visible process rather than an endpoint.

Failures can also be more directly integrated into successful publications. When appropriate, researchers can include clearly documented unsuccessful approaches alongside their final results, providing a more complete account of how conclusions were reached.

This model naturally encourages a more distributed form of collaboration. Instead of multiple teams attempting to replicate the same capabilities independently, researchers can specialise in specific techniques and contribute to shared problems. Experimental fields in particular would benefit, as access to calibrated equipment and technical expertise could be shared more efficiently. This reduces redundant investment and allows researchers, especially doctoral students, to focus on scientific questions rather than rebuilding infrastructure.

There have already been attempts in this direction, including initiatives at Utrecht University and elsewhere to share failed experiments. However, these efforts have remained limited due to career concerns and the additional effort required without clear incentives.

This again points to the need for systemic change. Alongside mandatory reporting, institutions could support modular publishing platforms that streamline submission, review and dissemination. Since researchers already perform most of the writing and reviewing, such systems could reduce reliance on traditional publishing structures and associated costs.

Accessibility must also be addressed. Publicly funded research should be publicly available. Paywalls restrict access not only to successful results but to the broader knowledge ecosystem. At a minimum, researchers should maintain open versions of their work through preprint platforms, ensuring that publicly funded knowledge remains accessible.

Reproducibility can be integrated into this system as well. Students and early-career researchers can contribute by attempting to reproduce published experiments and reporting their findings in a structured way. This strengthens reliability while providing valuable training and recognition.

To ensure coherence across these distributed contributions, more experienced researchers can take on a curatorial role. Rather than producing all components themselves, they can synthesise and connect results across teams, identify patterns, resolve inconsistencies and assemble broader narratives. Complete scientific stories would still exist, but they would emerge from the integration of many precisely documented parts rather than being produced in isolation.

Importantly, such a system can remain largely self-organising. Researchers already write, review and revise scientific work. Extending this to collaborative structuring and synthesis formalises roles that already exist informally within the academic community.

At a larger scale, this could evolve into a Failure Knowledge Commons. In Europe, such a system could be embedded within Horizon Europe, requiring projects above a defined funding threshold to submit “closed pathway reports” whenever research is discontinued or fails to confirm its original hypothesis. The United States is similarly well positioned to implement an equivalent system across its major funding agencies.

These reports would feed into a searchable platform organised by field, method, cause of failure, cost and stage of development. Researchers could quickly assess which approaches have already failed, under what conditions, and where alternative paths may still exist.

The system could also inform funding decisions. Grant reviewers could evaluate whether applicants have considered prior failed approaches. Over time, learning from dead ends could become as standard as reviewing existing literature.

Pilot programmes could be introduced across selected sectors such as health, energy and digital technologies and evaluated over several years. Metrics such as reduced duplication, improved proposal quality and more efficient allocation of resources could be used to assess impact.

As the database grows, it may also reveal broader structural issues. If many projects fail for similar reasons, this may point to systemic constraints such as limited access to specialised equipment, regulatory bottlenecks or coordination gaps between institutions. In this way, the platform would not only preserve knowledge from failure, but also help diagnose why progress slows.

Scientific research today is increasingly complex, specialised and costly. It cannot afford to repeatedly invest in the same mistakes. Public failure knowledge systems could turn wasted effort into shared progress and make research funding far more productive.

If the public pays for exploration, it should inherit the full map, including the dead ends. Those overlooked paths are not just remnants of failure. They are coordinates for where not to go next, and sometimes, clues for where to look differently.
